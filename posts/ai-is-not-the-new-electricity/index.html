AI Is Not the New Electricity

2018/04/22

This is my change request for the slogan to be: AI is the new EE. Maybe less marketable, but at least it fits on a hoodie, if you make AI and EE in big font. “AI is the new electricity” is kind of a mouthful, and as I will argue inaccurate. I will also attempt to show why phrasing this right is more important than some pedantic pet peeve, as it affects how people look at this emerging industry.

As a CS student, I have often asked myself what to focus on, and how does AI compare to the other new shiny fields? This essay is in part an attempt to answer some of these questions I have been asking myself, which is short for, I’m not trolling, just exploring opinions.

So, what is AI? If one flips open their dusty AIMA textbook, we can see there are 4 quadrants for Thinking vs Acting, Rationally vs Humanly.

No matter how you slice and dice it the common word is - Systems

System

A set of detailed methods, procedures and routines created to carry out a specific activity, perform a duty, or solve a problem

- http://www.businessdictionary.com/definition/system.html

Electricity is not a system, but I believe the motivation for associating electricity to AI is that at the end of the day their net economic effects are the same. Those effects are much discussed elsewhere. For the sake of brevity they are, in a sentence: increased automation, lowered production costs, increasing long term productivity, all that good stuff from econs, which also tells us poignantly that low wage jobs like some factory jobs will be lost.

But why conflate the effects and the processes of AI? It is the products of AI that are like electricity. AI/ML techniques are like generators, they convert raw data into a more useable resource, like predictions. The product is a percentage point (representing a prediction, classification, decision), which is a closer analogy to electricity, the only juice that my electronic drill needs, the only floating point value that an ad network needs to know to decide whether or not to send that ad to you.

AI/ML techniques transforms data into more usable forms.

The central problem in machine learning and deep learning is to meaningfully transform data: in other words, to learn useful representations of the input data at hand—representations that get us closer to the expected output … what’s a representation? At its core, it’s a different way to look at data — to represent or encode data.

- Deep Learning with Python, François Chollet

An alternative slogan could be: AI is the new transformer, which isn’t much more of a metaphor, but with confusing connotations it would at least prompt some thinking along those lines, although it leans more to the marketing side of the aforementioned accuracy-marketing tradeoff. Read the table below from left to right, AI is that one step before output, but all too often is lumped together with the rest of the columns.

Input Processed by Output Application Net Effect
Data (Audio, Video, Images, Text, etc.) AI/ML, Deep Learning Prediction, Classification, Action Ad network, Robot housekeepr Serve Ad, Clean room
Raw Material (Oil, Gas, Light, Waves, etc.) Generator / Engine, Turbine Electricity Electric Drill, Dishwasher Hole in wall, Clean dishes

Jumping the gun and thinking of AI in terms of its Applications & Net Effect (i.e. Products to sell) and not as a Process of Transforming data (i.e. as Generators) seems natural. By talking about AI and the end game in the same vein we necessarily associate the two. Perhaps there is no easy way to get around this, I mean I don’t even talk about electrical generators (unless one is going outfield into the jungle), now I just want to know if I have WIFI to type this thing. Maybe there are ways to dissociate this line of thinking, this essay is an attempt at such. Learning about AI/ML in school where one needs to write code (even if one doesn’t get the best grades) is, I think, the surest way to give people a concrete sense of how AI is affecting their lives in practice, which I guess is my roundabout way of hoping for more AI/ML courses to be publicly available.

There are only two hard things in Computer Science: cache invalidation and naming things.

- Phil Karlton

A final point. Words are like mental models attempting to approximate real meaning, even truth. I have put forward 2 alternatives I think have “better fit” than “electricity”, but hey, I may be wrong, it may even fall under the “True But Useless” category, we’ll only know after the testing.
